# Tone Guidelines

## Voice

Write like a technical peer who happens to work in sales — not a salesperson pretending to be technical. The reader is an engineering or data leader who deals with vendor pitches daily and can smell inauthenticity instantly.

- **Data-driven**: Lead with specifics (numbers, architectures, tool names), not vague claims
- **Concise**: Respect their time. Say it in fewer words.
- **Curious, not pushy**: Ask questions that show you understand their world. Don't assume their problems.
- **Peer-to-peer**: Talk like you're an engineer at a meetup, not a rep on a sales call
- **Grounded**: Reference real technical concepts (metrics layers, dbt models, observability pipelines) — never bluff

## Do

- Keep cold emails under 120 words (shorter is almost always better)
- Use the recipient's first name
- Reference something specific: a job posting, blog post, tech stack detail, or recent funding round
- Use technical language accurately (e.g., "metrics layer" not "data solution")
- Write in lowercase subject lines (feels less like marketing)
- Use short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each)
- End with a low-friction ask ("worth a 15-min look?" not "let's schedule a 45-minute deep dive")
- Match their energy — if they write casually, respond casually

## Don't

- Use buzzwords: "synergy," "leverage," "holistic," "cutting-edge," "revolutionary"
- Start with "I hope this email finds you well" or "I wanted to reach out because"
- Use exclamation marks (one per email maximum, and only if genuinely warranted)
- Name-drop customers without context (saying "we work with Stripe" means nothing without explaining relevance)
- Write walls of text — if it looks long, it won't get read
- Use "we" more than "you" — the email should be about them, not Metricflow
- Send templated messages without personalization — every recipient should feel like the email was written for them
- Use phrases like "pick your brain" or "I'd love to learn more about your role"
- Include links in the first cold email (triggers spam filters and feels salesy)

## Good Examples

**Good cold email opening (ICP 1 — VP Eng at SaaS):**
> Saw your team's post about migrating off a custom metrics pipeline — sounds like that was a heavy lift. Curious if you've looked at standardizing metric definitions across your monitoring stack, or if that's still a manual process?

**Good follow-up (short, new angle):**
> One thing I forgot to mention — we just published a case study with a Series C company that cut their Datadog bill by 35% after consolidating metric definitions through Metricflow. Thought it might be relevant given your scale.

**Good LinkedIn DM:**
> Hey Sarah — your talk at DataCouncil about metric governance really resonated. We're building tooling in exactly that space. Would love to compare notes if you're open to it.

## Bad Examples

**Bad cold email opening:**
> Hi! I hope this finds you well. I'm reaching out because Metricflow is a revolutionary analytics platform that leverages cutting-edge technology to transform how companies think about their data infrastructure. I'd love to schedule a call to discuss how we can help your team!

Why it's bad: Generic opener, buzzword-heavy, no personalization, no evidence of research, aggressive CTA, multiple exclamation marks.

**Bad follow-up:**
> Just checking in on my last email. Did you have a chance to review it? Let me know if you'd like to connect.

Why it's bad: Adds zero value, makes the prospect feel guilty, no new information or angle.
