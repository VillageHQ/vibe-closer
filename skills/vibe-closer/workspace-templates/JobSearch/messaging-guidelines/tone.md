# Tone Guidelines

## Voice
- **Confident but not entitled** -- You know your value and can articulate it clearly, but you're not above proving it. Confidence comes from specific accomplishments, not vague claims.
- **Professional but personable** -- Professional context, genuine human connection. You're a real person with real interests, not a resume in human form.
- **Enthusiastic but genuine** -- Real, specific interest in their mission and product. Not generic "I'm passionate about..." excitement that could apply to any company.
- **Knowledgeable but humble** -- Show you've done homework on their product, market, and challenges without being pedantic or presumptuous about their internal dynamics.
- **Direct and clear** -- Respect their time. Get to the point. Say what you want and why in the fewest words possible.

## Do
- Keep emails under 100 words (shorter = higher reply rate)
- Use the recipient's first name
- Reference something specific about the company -- a recent product launch, blog post, funding round, or feature you've used
- Connect their needs to your experience with a concrete, quantified example
- Ask a genuine question (not a rhetorical or leading one)
- Be authentic -- mention if you use their product, if you know someone there, if their recent blog post changed how you think about something
- Include a clear, low-friction next step ("Would you have 15 minutes next week?")
- Tailor your tone to the recipient's role: casual with PMs, direct with recruiters, strategic with executives
- Show you've used or studied their product -- nothing signals genuine interest more than product-specific observations

## Don't
- Open with "I hope this email finds you well" or any generic pleasantry
- Lead with what you want ("I'm looking for a role...") -- lead with what you bring and why you're interested in them
- Use corporate buzzwords ("synergy," "leverage," "passionate," "best-of-breed")
- Make it about you -- make it about them and mutual fit
- Overuse exclamation marks (max 1 per email)
- Be too casual if the company or role is in a traditional industry (read the room)
- Copy-paste the same email to different people -- personalization is visible and its absence is even more visible
- Apologize for reaching out ("Sorry to bother you...") -- if they're hiring, you're adding value by being a strong candidate
- List your skills without context -- "I'm great at product strategy" means nothing without a specific example
- Attach your resume in a cold email -- offer to share it if they're interested
- Name-drop former employers without connecting the experience to their specific needs

## Examples of Good Tone

### Cold Email to Hiring Manager
```
Hi Sarah,

I've been using Linear for the past year and your recent launch of Triage
is exactly how I think about developer workflow tools -- opinionated defaults
that get out of the way.

At Notion, I led the API platform from private beta to 50K+ active
integrations. I'm exploring Head of Product roles at developer tools
companies and would love to learn more about what you're building next.

Would you have 15 minutes next week?

Best,
Jordan
```

### Warm Intro Request
```
Hi Marcus,

You mentioned you know the team at Raycast. I'm exploring product leadership
roles in developer tools, and their approach to extensibility is genuinely
impressive. Would you be comfortable making an intro? Happy to send you a
blurb to forward.

Thanks,
Jordan
```

## Adjustment by Recipient Role

**Hiring Manager/VP of Product**: Lead with product insight and strategic thinking. Show you understand their product challenges. Be specific about why their role (not just their company) excites you.

**Recruiter**: Be clear and efficient about what you're looking for. State your target role, level, and what makes you a strong candidate in 2-3 sentences. Make it easy for them to match you.

**Internal Employee (Networking)**: Show genuine interest in their work and experience. Ask for advice and perspective, not a job. Build the relationship first -- the referral follows naturally.

**CEO/Founder**: Lead with strategic perspective. Reference their company's market position and where you see product opportunity. Keep it brief and high-conviction.

## Red Flags to Avoid

- Typos or grammar errors in any outreach (proofread everything)
- Overselling with superlatives ("I'm the best PM you'll find")
- Generic praise that could apply to any company ("I love your mission")
- Subject lines that look like spam or clickbait
- Asking too much in the first message (the goal is a conversation, not an interview)
- Mentioning compensation or title expectations in initial outreach
- Badmouthing a former employer or explaining why you left
