# BD & Partnership Tone Guidelines

## Voice

- **Peer-to-peer**: We're equals exploring mutual opportunity, not a vendor pitching a buyer. Partnerships only work when both sides see value.
- **Collaborative**: Frame everything as "let's explore together" not "here's what we want from you." Use "we" language that includes both companies.
- **Strategic**: Focus on business logic, market fit, and shared customers -- not feature lists or product demos.
- **Mutual-value focused**: Every message should make clear what's in it for them. If you can't articulate their upside, you're not ready to reach out.
- **Informed**: Show you've done real homework on their product, market position, and recent activity. Generic outreach kills BD conversations.

## Do
- Lead with a specific insight about their product or company -- show you've spent time researching
- Articulate the exact collaboration idea (e.g., "surface your monitoring data in our service catalog" not "explore partnership")
- Keep emails under 150 words -- partnerships are explored in calls, not email threads
- Use their first name and personalize beyond just swapping in the company name
- Propose a specific, low-commitment next step (30-min intro call)
- Reference complementary strengths -- frame it as 1+1=3, not "we need you"
- Show respect for their decision timeline; never pressure for speed
- Mention shared customers or overlapping use cases if you know them
- Reference a recent announcement, product launch, or blog post to show timeliness

## Don't
- Use buzzwords like "synergy," "best-of-breed," "game-changer," "unlock value," or "strategic alignment"
- Start with "I hope this email finds you well" -- get to the point
- Make it about you: "We're looking for partners" is boring and self-serving
- Oversell or exaggerate your customer base, reach, or traction
- Assume they know your product -- briefly explain what DevStack does in context
- Be vague about partnership type: what kind? what does the first milestone look like?
- Copy-paste templates without real personalization -- partnership emails must feel custom
- Pitch in the first message -- the goal is an exploratory conversation, not a close
- Send the same message to the VP of Partnerships and the Director of Product -- tailor to their role
- Use "just checking in" as a follow-up -- always bring new information or a new angle

## Examples of Good Tone

**Good**: "We noticed your team just shipped a new Kubernetes integration. DevStack surfaces service docs and ownership alongside infra context -- our customers keep asking for observability data inline. Thought there might be a natural fit to explore."

**Bad**: "We're excited about partnership opportunities and think a synergy between our platforms would be game-changing for the developer experience market."

**Good**: "A lot of our shared customers use your CI/CD alongside DevStack. We've been thinking about surfacing build status in our service catalog. Curious if that's something your integrations team has explored."

**Bad**: "We'd love to partner and leverage your distribution network to accelerate our growth."

**Good**: "Saw your CEO's post about the platform engineering trend. We're seeing the same thing from our side. Wondered if it's worth a 30-minute conversation about how our products fit together for those teams."

**Bad**: "DevStack is the leading developer portal platform and we think integrating with your product would be mutually beneficial for both organizations."
